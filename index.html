What is SDG 13?

Take urgent action
to combat climate change.

The United Nations' 13th Sustainable Development Goal calls on every nation, city, and individual to act on climate change before 2030. It focuses on strengthening resilience to climate disasters, integrating climate policy into national planning, and mobilizing $100 billion annually in climate finance for developing countries. The goal is ambitious — and the progress, while incomplete, is real.

Dataset Visualizations

Five datasets.
One clear trend.

Data drawn from the IEA, Global Carbon Project, UN SDG Portal, and Enerdata. Each chart tells a piece of a hopeful story — slower emissions growth, surging renewables, and decoupling of wealth from pollution.

Dataset 1 · Global Carbon Project / IEA
Global CO₂ Emissions Growth Is Slowing
Annual fossil fuel CO₂ emissions (billion tonnes). While emissions still rise, the rate of growth has dropped dramatically — from +3% yearly in the 2000s to under 1% recently. Advanced economies are now declining.
Dataset 2 · Enerdata / IEA
Renewables Share of Global Electricity
Renewables grew from ~20% of global electricity in 2010 to 32% in 2024 — a 60% increase in share. Solar and wind led the charge.
Dataset 3 · IEA Advanced Economy Data
Decoupling: GDP Up, Emissions Down
In advanced economies from 2013–2023, GDP grew +17% while CO₂ emissions fell -15%. Economic growth no longer requires pollution growth.
Dataset 4 · UN Standing Committee on Finance
Climate Finance Is Surging
Global climate finance flows reached $1.3 trillion/year in 2021–22, a 63% increase from 2019–20. Transport, clean energy, and buildings are the biggest recipients.
Dataset 5 · Wikipedia / Ember / IEA 2024
Share of Electricity from Renewables by Region (2024)
Europe leads with 51% of its electricity from renewables. Latin America benefits from massive hydropower. North America and Asia are catching up fast — China alone added nearly 60% of all new global renewable capacity in 2024.

Dataset Context & Explanation

What the data
actually means.

Why CO₂ Emissions Are Still Rising — But the Story Is Changing

Global fossil fuel CO₂ hit 37.4 billion tonnes in 2024 — a record. But the rate of growth has slowed dramatically. In the early 2000s, emissions grew by 3% per year. The last decade averaged under 0.6%. Without clean energy technology deployed since 2019, global emissions would have been three times higher. The flattening trend is the early signal of a genuine turn. Advanced economies (EU, US, UK) are now in sustained decline — their emissions are at 50-year lows even as their economies grow.

Source: IEA CO₂ Report 2024 · Global Carbon Budget 2024

The Renewable Electricity Revolution and What It Signals

In 2024, renewables supplied 32% of global electricity, up from just 20% in 2010. That shift is driven almost entirely by solar and wind — technologies whose costs have fallen by 90% in a decade. In Europe, renewables crossed 50% of electricity in 2024. In Chile, solar alone provides 23% of the grid. The IEA reports that wind and solar accounted for more than half of all new electricity generated globally in 2024. At current growth rates, renewables could reach 50–70% of global electricity by 2050 — making the 1.5°C target achievable if policy keeps pace.

Source: IEA Global Energy Review 2025 · Enerdata 2024 · RFF Global Energy Outlook 2026

Limiting warming to 1.5°C instead of 2°C prevents hundreds of millions of people from being exposed to climate-related risks. The choices made this decade will shape the next century.

What You Can Do

The invitation
to act.

This data isn't just interesting — it's a call to action. Progress is real, but it isn't inevitable. Here's where you fit in.

For Individuals
Make Energy Personal

Switch to a green energy provider. Reduce meat consumption by 50%. Choose public transit or EV. Your choices aggregate into the data above — the 32% renewable share is made of millions of individual and policy decisions.

For Communities
Demand Local Policy

Attend city council meetings. Advocate for building efficiency codes, public transit investment, and green procurement. Cities account for 70% of global emissions — and cities respond to organized residents.

For Students & Professionals
Build the Clean Economy

14 million people now work in renewable energy — a number doubling this decade. Energy informatics, climate finance, sustainable architecture, and environmental policy are among the fastest-growing career paths of the 2030s.
